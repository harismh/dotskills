## Core model

Formulation has four layers:

1. **Purpose:** select knowledge that serves a real project, decision, capability, curiosity, or assessment.
2. **Understanding:** build a coherent local model before asking memory to preserve it.
3. **Representation:** create small retrieval paths through that model.
4. **Revision:** repair cards when later context or review failures expose a bad representation.

Think of concepts as nodes and useful prompts as retrieval edges. Atomicity applies to each graded target; richness belongs to the network of cards.

## Selection and understanding

- Ask what durable recall will enable. Material learned for an active purpose tends to yield better questions than undirected stockpiling.
- Extract selectively. Favor core claims, distinctions, mechanisms, causal relationships, fluent terminology, high-value examples, limitations, and prerequisites.
- Do not formulate what the learner cannot yet explain. Expand terms, trace the argument, or defer the passage.
- Build a simple picture of the whole before fragmenting it. For unfamiliar or large sources, use repeated passes: map the source, learn easy foundations, then revisit deeper material.
- Do not finalize a passage while later context may redefine or qualify it. Keep tentative formulations in a scratchpad.
- Treat card construction itself as elaborative encoding. Ask the learner to choose priorities, explain ambiguous ideas, supply connections, and critique drafts.
- Avoid lonely orphan facts. Connect a new idea to at least one useful distinction, implication, example, prerequisite, or neighboring concept when possible.
- Stop when marginal material is less valuable than the future review cost. Thoroughness is a choice, not a default.

## Incremental processing funnel

Use this lifecycle for large, unfamiliar, or uneven material:

`source → triage → temporary extract → clarify and refine → card or discard`

- Survey the whole source before deep formulation so partial reading does not destroy global context.
- Triage coherent units as `discard`, `defer`, or `extract`. Selection marks a candidate, not a card.
- Give a deferred unit a reason and a revisit condition. Deferral without a prerequisite, changed priority, or next action becomes procrastination.
- Keep a compact backlog record for each temporary extract: source span, purpose, priority, prerequisite or blocker, and next action.
- Process the highest-value coherent slice first. Reprioritize after every pass because later context can change the value or meaning of earlier extracts.
- Promote an extract only after it is understood, contextualized, source-faithful, and objectively gradable. A quick cloze must pass the same gate as Q&A.
- Preserve lineage from source to extract to card so provenance and hierarchical relationships survive decomposition.
- End each checkpoint with queue state: processed, card-ready, deferred, discarded, blockers, and next slice.
- Periodically prune the backlog and synthesize across slices. Incremental processing must not fragment the learner's model of the whole.
- Collapse the lifecycle for short, simple material when its overhead would exceed its value.

Do not encode the archived article's dramatic speed, retention, adoption, or mechanistic claims as skill rules. Its useful contribution here is workflow structure, not evidence of effect size.

## Atomicity and grading

- Test one thing at a time. If a learner could recall part of an answer and miss another part, split the card.
- Do not equate atomicity with one card per sentence fragment. A short set may remain one schema-level target when whole-set recall is useful and the requested card budget is compact.
- Keep the required response minimal. Put optional explanation after the core answer, not inside the success criterion.
- Atomic does not mean elementary. A sophisticated question can be atomic when its prerequisites are already fluent.
- Keep a larger synthesis card only when smaller cards cover its components. Use it to test collation, not first-line memorization.
- Omit microcards whose cue almost states the answer, whose fact is incidental, or whose only purpose is mechanical source coverage.
- Phrase the prompt so a correct answer is identifiable before reveal. If several answers are acceptable, state the criterion or list the accepted equivalence succinctly.
- Use stable context to disambiguate a domain, source, period, jurisdiction, version, or sense. Do not add decorative context that becomes an artificial cue.
- Treat yes/no prompts as a refactoring smell. Ask what, why, how, which condition, which example, or which contrast instead.

## Useful retrieval paths

Generate only paths that matter:

- **Term → meaning** and, when useful, **meaning → term**.
- **Notation → meaning** and, when production matters, **meaning → notation**.
- **Formal statement**, **informal intuition**, and **worked implication** as separate targets.
- **Cause → effect**, **effect → likely cause**, and **condition → consequence** when each direction supports use.
- **Parent → children** and non-obvious **child → parent** for hierarchies.
- **Concept A vs. concept B** for likely interference.
- **Principle → example** and **example → principle** when transfer matters.
- **Atomic parts → integrative whole** for synthesis.

Redundancy across simple cards can strengthen valuable knowledge. Redundancy within one graded answer makes scheduling and grading worse.

## Cloze, sets, and sequences

- Use cloze for a meaningful phrase in a useful sentence, exact language, notation in context, or a local sequence transition.
- Do not delete an arbitrary word merely because it is easy to mark. The hidden span must be the intended retrieval target.
- Keep a cloze small enough to grade. When several blanks are independent, give them different Mochi group indices or split them into cards.
- Avoid large unordered sets. Replace them with membership, grouping, causal, historical, or hierarchical relations.
- Ask for a whole list only when recalling the whole is genuinely useful. Separate the list from explanations of its members.
- For an exact sequence, prefer local predecessor/successor or overlapping-context prompts, optionally backed by one whole-sequence test. Do not generate every direction unless the sequence deserves that review cost.
- For a poem, quotation, or procedure whose order matters, cue the next small unit from one or two preceding units and retain a whole performance outside the cards.

## Interference and associations

- Similar terms, dates, people, values, formulas, and cases can become mutually confusable. Use explicit contrast cards and discriminating features.
- Optimize wording so the intended memory activates quickly. Remove incidental prose that is not learned for free.
- Refer to already-known concepts when they clarify the cue and reduce ambiguity.
- Invite a personal, vivid, or concrete example when it genuinely fixes the semantics. Do not make recall depend on a cue unavailable where the knowledge will be used.
- Use imagery for inherently visual or spatial knowledge. Use mnemonics mainly for stubborn arbitrary material; do not let them replace understanding.
- Expect interference to emerge later. A difficult or repeatedly failed card is a candidate for splitting, contrasting, rewording, or deletion.

## Truth, provenance, and time

- Verify a new inference before recording it as knowledge.
- Phrase disputed or weakly checked material as a named source's claim rather than as universal fact.
- Preserve a source alongside the card by default when reliability, updating, or disagreement may matter. Do not test the source unless attribution is part of the knowledge.
- Attach a date, software version, jurisdiction, population, or other scope marker to volatile facts. Make it part of the prompt only when it is needed to select the correct answer.
- Surface contradictions instead of silently normalizing them.

## Declarative and procedural knowledge

Cards can preserve commands, cues, steps, failure modes, and reasons. They cannot by themselves create procedural fluency. Pair cards with authentic execution for code, mathematics, diagnosis, writing, physical actions, performance, or other skills.

## High-value pushback

Choose only the question that changes the formulation:

- What will remembering this let you do?
- Which part can you already explain in your own words?
- Is the target the exact wording, the underlying idea, or both?
- What precision is actually required?
- Is this settled fact or this source's claim?
- Could more than one answer count as correct?
- Do you need the whole list, and does order matter?
- Which two ideas here are most likely to be confused?
- What prerequisite or concrete example would make this connect?
- What real task will you practice alongside these procedural cards?

## Failure smells

Refactor or reject:

- opaque source text converted without comprehension;
- cards generated to cover every sentence rather than a learning purpose;
- several independent facts in one answer;
- copied paragraphs with arbitrary cloze marks;
- prompts answerable from accidental grammatical or visual cues;
- ambiguous prompts that admit an unknown set of answers;
- raw sets, long enumerations, or exhaustive sequence expansion;
- automatic reversals whose answer is trivial;
- isolated trivia with no useful conceptual neighbor;
- source assertions promoted to fact without qualification;
- changing facts without a date or version;
- declarative cards presented as sufficient practice;
- large batches emitted without learner involvement;
- temporary extracts promoted directly to cards without a readiness check;
- deferred material with no reason, revisit condition, or pruning;
- incremental fragments that are never reconciled into a whole;
- editorial notes or uncertainties left inside importable cards.

## Reconcile apparent tensions

- **Whole first vs. incremental learning:** require a provisional whole and local understanding, then allow early foundational cards to deepen the whole over successive passes.
- **Atomicity vs. synthesis:** build atomic foundations and retain a few supported integrative cards.
- **Minimum information vs. rich encoding:** minimize each tested response while adding useful examples, contrasts, and reverse directions as separate cards.
- **Cloze speed vs. Q&A quality:** choose the representation that matches the retrieval goal; do not make either universal.
- **Context vs. transfer:** include enough context to disambiguate, but only cues likely to exist when the knowledge is needed.
- **Source metadata vs. learned attribution:** keep the source outside the required answer unless who claimed it changes the proposition.
